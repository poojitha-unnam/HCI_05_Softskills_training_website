About the Course

Conflict resolution is the process of resolving a dispute or a conflict by meeting at least some of each side’s needs and addressing their interests. This course will help you develop the skills needed to manage and resolve conflicts effectively, whether in the workplace, at home, or in other settings.

Course Topics

1. Understanding Conflict
Learn the basics of conflict, including its sources and types. Conflict, arguments, and change are natural parts of our lives, as well as the lives of every agency, organization, and nation. Conflict resolution is a way for two or more parties to find a peaceful solution to a disagreement among them. The disagreement may be personal, financial, political, or emotional. When a dispute arises, often the best course of action is negotiation to resolve the disagreement. The goals of negotiation are: To produce a solution that all parties can agree to To work as quickly as possible to find this solution To improve, not hurt, the relationship between the groups in conflict Conflict resolution through negotiation can be good for all parties involved. Often, each side will get more by participating in negotiations than they would by walking away, and it can be a way for your group to get resources that might otherwise be out of reach.

2. Communication Skills
Enhance your communication skills to express yourself clearly and listen effectively during conflicts. Communication is more than the words we choose to use. In fact, our body language and tone of voice often speak louder than our words. For example, shouting “I’m not angry” is not a very convincing message! When we give an incongruent message where our tone of voice and body language does not match our message, confusion and frustration often follow (Gottman & DeClaire, 2001). In order to overcome this communication challenge, we need to be aware of what messages our body language and tone of voice may be sending others. Speak calmly, give eye contact, smile when appropriate, and maintain an open and relaxed posture

3. Conflict Resolution Styles
Explore different styles of conflict resolution and understand when to use each. Here are some ways to resolve conflict: Communicate directly: Talk to the person you're having a problem with, instead of complaining to others or sending a letter. Plan ahead: Set aside enough time for a thorough discussion. Listen: Give the other person a chance to share their side of the story. Avoid blame: 't name-call or antagonize the other person. Focus on the issue: Agree on the problem, and generate multiple solutions. Establish a common goal: Make sure everyone involved has the same vision. Follow up: Agree on a schedule to check in and make sure the agreement is working. Be emotionally aware: Try to understand your own feelings and those of others. Use neutral questions: Ask questions to help defuse the conflict and move it towards collaboration. Point out assumptions: Instead of criticizing an idea, ask if the assumptions behind it are true.

4. Negotiation Techniques
Discover techniques to negotiate and reach mutually beneficial agreements. Negotiation is a strategic communication process that can be used to resolve conflicts by finding a mutually satisfactory agreement. It's a popular method for conflict resolution because it aims to respect each party's perspective while minimizing the negative impact of the disagreement. Here are some tips for negotiating a conflict resolution: Plan: Before negotiating, consider your goals, interests, and positions. You can also try to anticipate the best, fair, and minimally acceptable resolutions. Be clear: Make sure you're clear about what's important to you, and try to understand the other party's goals and issues. Be open to compromise: Negotiation often involves compromise and concessions. Be an active listener: Ask questions and try to understand the other party's perspective. Consider value creation: Look for opportunities to capitalize on shared interests or noncompetitive similarities. You can also try trading on differing preferences and priorities. Consider confidentiality: If both parties might suffer reputational damage if the dispute goes public, they might agree to keep some aspects of the process confidential.

5. Emotional Intelligence
Understand the role of emotional intelligence in managing conflicts. Emotional intelligence (EI) can help you resolve conflict by helping you understand and manage your own emotions and those of others. Here are some ways you can use EI to resolve conflict: Self-awareness: Recognize your emotional responses and how they affect your views and behaviors. Self-regulation: Manage your emotions and inner resources, and express them appropriately. Empathy: Understand and share the feelings of others. Social awareness: See and respect differences, pick up on emotional cues, and recognize when someone is in an elevated emotional state. Active listening: Demonstrate that you understand what the other person is saying and how they are feeling about it. Relationship management: Build trust, collaborate, and communicate effectively. Here are some tips for using EI to resolve conflict: Make calm statements of fact. Ask questions. Try to understand the other person's point of view without judgment. Explain the outcome you are hoping for and ask for other ideas for solutions

6. Problem-Solving Skills
Learn how to approach conflicts with a problem-solving mindset. Problem solving is a key part of conflict resolution, which is the ability to identify and manage conflict in a constructive way. Here are some strategies for resolving conflict using problem solving: Acknowledge the conflict: Recognize that there is a problem and agree on reaching a resolution. Communicate effectively: Good communication can prevent conflicts and help others understand complex concepts. Actively listen: Try to understand the other person's perspective and feelings. Collaborate: Analyze each other's interests to find a solution that meets everyone's needs. Compromise: Agree to partially satisfy each other's needs or interests. Identify the root causes: Understand the different perspectives of those involved. Bring the parties together: Discuss the conflict with the people involved. Monitor and follow up: Make sure the solution is working.

7. Mediation and Facilitation
Discover methods for mediating and facilitating discussions to resolve conflicts. Mediation is a process that can help resolve conflicts by facilitating communication and negotiation between parties. A neutral third party, called a mediator, helps the parties reach a voluntary resolution without resorting to force or law. Mediation can be an effective way to resolve conflicts when: Parties are having difficulty resolving the dispute Parties are resistant to confronting the other party Here are some characteristics of mediation: Neutral location: The mediator meets with the parties at a neutral location where they can discuss the dispute. Confidential: Everything said in mediation is confidential, and participants can't discuss the content with others. Participants control the outcome: The participants, not the mediator, control the outcome of the mediation. Win-win resolution: The goal is to reach a voluntary resolution that is acceptable to all parties. Creative solutions: Participants can collaboratively design creative solutions
